# Marketing Claims vs Technical Reality

Side-by-side comparison of what CryptoTab advertises vs what OSINT establishes.

| # | Marketing claim (with source) | Technical / OSINT-confirmed reality |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **"Mine BTC while you browse"** — top of cryptotabbrowser.com | The software mines **Monero (XMR)** on the user's CPU and converts to BTC at the live exchange rate at withdrawal time. Stated in the company's own FAQ: *"CryptoTab software mines Monero (XMR) and converts Monero to Bitcoin according to current exchange rate"* (https://cryptobrowser.site/en/faq/mining/mine-other-cryptocurrencies/). Real BTC mining on a consumer CPU is mathematically impossible. |
| 2 | **"More than 35 million users from more than 220 countries"** — cryptotabbrowser.com | This figure is unaudited and self-reported. The Estonian operator declares **only 2 employees in Q1 2026** (inforegister.ee). The "35M users" figure is not used for any auditable revenue/EBITDA disclosure; it appears only in marketing. |
| 3 | **"Average monthly income of CryptoTab users equal to $4 USD in BTC"** — used in the on-site earnings calculator (https://cryptotabbrowser.com/) | The realistic CPU-mining yield of Monero RandomX at typical hashrates (M1 ≈ 5 KH/s; mid-range Intel/AMD ≈ 5–9 KH/s) is **$0.30–$5/month gross**, of which the operator captures a majority share before payout. The $4 figure is plausible only after operator cross-subsidy from Cloud.Boost revenue (see `payout-economics.md`). |
| 4 | **"Basic speed: 300 H/s (Lite), 1500 H/s (Pro), 2000 H/s (Max)"** — cryptotabbrowser.com | These are the **displayed in-app rates**, not measured RandomX hashes. A real Monero hashrate from a typical user CPU is on the order of 2,000–9,000 H/s, so these figures are within the right order of magnitude — but Pro/Max are paid tiers; the user has to pay to access rates a free RandomX miner would get without paying. |
| 5 | **"1500× faster mining with Cloud.Boost"** / multipliers x2, x5, x10, x15 — https://cryptobrowser.site/en/cloudboost/ | Cloud.Boost is explicitly marketed as **"unlimited speed without power consumption"** — i.e., it is **not** doing more cryptographic work on the user's hardware. It is an in-account multiplier on a UI counter. Whether the operator backs the multiplier with real owned/rented hashpower is not publicly disclosed. From a payout-funding standpoint, Cloud.Boost behaves as a **paid subscription** whose "earnings" are funded by Cloud.Boost subscription revenue, not by additional mining. |
| 6 | **"Get a stable BTC income"** — cryptotabbrowser.com homepage | Trustpilot, Sitejabber, Reddit, Google Play, and Medium all document a consistent pattern of **small withdrawals being honored** and **large withdrawals being slow-rolled or denied** under a generic "Terms of Use violation" clause. Specific documented cases: 8-month pending, 1.5-year pending (0.00245 BTC), 7.5-month "Processing" stuck, account terminations after Cloud.Boost purchase. Income is "stable" only at the sub-threshold level the operator chooses to honor. |
| 7 | **"15% recurring commission"** — affiliate program landing https://cryptobrowser.site/en/affiliate/ | Accurate **for Level 1 only**. The 10-level structure decays geometrically: 15% / 10% / 5% / 3% / 2% / 1% / 0.5% / 0.25% / 0.125% / 0.0625%. Affiliates promoting the "15% recurring" headline omit that >90% of the curve's value is in the top two levels. |
| 8 | **"Mining network up to 10 levels of referrals"** — affiliate page | True. This is a **textbook MLM unilevel compensation plan**. The recruitment funnel is the dominant user-acquisition mechanism and the dominant economic mechanism — most operator revenue does not come from individual miners' CPU work, it comes from those miners' purchases of Cloud.Boost and Pro/Max tier upgrades. |
| 9 | **"No investments required"** — affiliate program copy | Technically true (the browser is free). But the **product economically optimized** by the operator — the one the affiliate program incentivizes — is Cloud.Boost, which **does require BTC payment**. The marketing routes free users into an investment ladder. |
| 10 | **"Withdraw at minimum 0.00001 BTC, no commissions"** — FAQ | The threshold is accurate. The "no commissions" statement is accurate for the withdrawal itself. What the FAQ omits is that **withdrawals can be placed in indefinite Pending or accounts can be terminated** under the open-ended *"violation of CryptoTab Browser terms of use in any manner"* clause that is also in the FAQ. |
| 11 | **"Stable, secure, fast" Chromium-based browser** — product positioning | The desktop binary IS a real Chromium fork, Apple-notarized (Team ID `46J7LAWF9F`), code-signed, and functional as a browser. This part of the product is genuine. The mining and affiliate layer sits on top of an otherwise legitimate Chromium build, which is precisely why anti-malware tools don't flag it and why platform stores keep it published. |
| 12 | **Cryptocompany OÜ, Estonia — EU regulated** (implicit via "Estonia" branding) | Estonia is in the EU but **the company holds no VASP license**. Its EMTAK code 62901 classifies it as "Other IT services" — non-financial. It has €2,500 share capital, 2 declared employees, current tax debt of €15.6K, and a "Risky" credit rating per inforegister.ee. It is legally registered but operates entirely outside the consumer-finance regulatory perimeter. |

## What this means in plain English

CryptoTab Browser is **not a scam in the simple sense** — the browser works, mining of Monero genuinely happens, and small withdrawals genuinely complete. But every load-bearing marketing claim has a softer truth behind it:

- "Mine BTC" → actually mines Monero, converts to BTC at withdrawal.
- "Stable income" → stable for small balances, unstable for large ones.
- "1500× faster mining" → 1500× a number on a screen, not 1500× the cryptographic work.
- "EU-based, Estonia" → an unlicensed €2,500-capital shell with 2 employees and tax debt.

The product is **engineered to look honest at the entry point** (real Chromium fork, real small BTC payouts) so that real users build real referral networks — which is the actual product the operator is selling.
